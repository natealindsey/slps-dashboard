Why I built this

I ran these numbers as a parent, not an analyst. My two kids attend Meramec Elementary in Dutchtown (95% Black, 64% of students directly certified, 2024-25 NCES), one of the schools slated to close under every model. One is an identified gifted student; both are exceptional kids at an exceptional school, with teachers and a community that are, plainly, amazing. Nothing in this data changes that.

But loving your school is not the same as defending the system it sits in. The district's own Preview names Community Impact as its first planning question and then never measures it. So I did. What the data shows is a district where poverty and racial isolation track each other almost perfectly, where the closure list lands hardest on the poorest and most segregated schools, and where the best resourced pathways serve the fewest poor children. Meramec is an exceptional school inside a system that needs reforming, and the point of reform has to be that ALL students in St. Louis attend well-resourced public schools.

One thing this data forced me to accept: in a city as racially segregated as ours, there is no plan that avoids closing predominantly Black and poor schools. The students are where the students are, and most SLPS buildings serve them. So the question is not whether a plan touches Black and poor schools; every plan will. The question is whether the district is willing to attempt something harder: a new structural plan whose impact is less disproportionate and that builds the basis of a system that reverses our self-segregating tendencies. That attempt requires a long term analysis of the two-tier system we already operate, in which the district's most resourced students, who are disproportionately White, cluster in a handful of gifted-only schools while everyone else's schools are consolidated around them. You cannot build an equitable system by reorganizing only one of its tiers.

Read the data, then tell the district what you think.

Nate Lindsey · Meramec Elementary parent, Dutchtown

What "Model D" means on this page. Models A, B, and C are the district's three draft scenarios. Model D is not a district proposal. It is a hypothetical this analysis constructs as a sensitivity test [a what-if scenario run purely to see how the numbers change]: everything Model C does, plus closing the three gifted-only buildings the plan protects (Betty Wheeler, Mallinckrodt, and McKinley). Its purpose is diagnostic. Because roughly a third of the district's White students and an eighth of its non-poor students attend just those three schools, adding them to the closure list flips the plan's racial disparity and erases its poverty disparity, which shows that the equity footprint of Future Ready SLPS is driven by which schools are treated as untouchable, not by which buildings are empty. Model D is a lens for reading the plan, not a recommendation to close those schools.

The plan, generally

The "Future Ready SLPS: School Portfolio Preview" (Dr. Myra Berry, June 29, 2026) is a draft discussion document, explicitly not a final recommendation. Its driver is enrollment collapse: the district has lost roughly 58% of its students since 1991 and projects a further 6% decline by 2026-27. All three models point the same direction: fewer, fuller, better-staffed buildings. Every remaining school is promised a minimum staffing floor (4 related-arts teachers, academic coach, AP, social worker, counselor, nurse, behavior support), and every model preserves the specialized pathways: VPA, Advanced Academics/gifted, early childhood, CTE, and separate SPED sites. The Preview names "Community Impact" as the first of its five planning questions but never assesses it; measuring who the closures actually land on, by race and by poverty, is the purpose of this dashboard.

The equity picture at a glance

  • Who attends the closing schools: the 19 Model C closures are 86.9% Black (DESE 2025) against a 75.5% Black district, and 65.1% directly certified against a 52.7% district rate.
  • Concentration: 16 of 19 closing schools are at least 90% Black; 16 of 19 sit above the district poverty rate; the pending pair (Hickey/Lexington) is 100% Black and 74% directly certified.
  • Relative likelihood: a Black student is 2.0x as likely as a White student, and a deep-poverty student 1.67x as likely as a non-poor student, to have their school closed.
  • What is protected: the gifted pathway retained in every model holds the district's five whitest and five least-poor student bodies.
  • The two exceptions prove the rule: Model C's two unique closures, Buder and Mann, are the district's most racially diverse neighborhood schools, and that fact deserves honest acknowledgment. But they are exceptions within a list, not a counterweight to it: the other seventeen closing schools are 94.6% Black with 71.2% of students in deep poverty, and even Buder and Mann are majority students of color with more than a third of their students directly certified. The closure list is not diverse; it contains two diverse schools.

The three models

Every model shrinks the district from 62 schools to roughly 40-47, and the core closure list of ~16 majority-Black schools is common to all three, which means the equity findings on this dashboard apply to every model, not just Model C. What separates the models is how aggressively they consolidate and who bears the increment: Model C is the most aggressive and the only model that reaches into the diverse South Side schools (Buder and Mann). Model C is the model all closure figures here reference.

Poverty and the closure list

SLPS is a Community Eligibility district, so every school reports 100% free lunch; direct certification (automatic certification via SNAP, TANF, foster, homeless, or migrant status) is the real school-level poverty measure. The Model C closure list concentrates on the district's poorest buildings: the 19 closures run 65.1% direct certification against a 52.7% district rate, and the district's three poorest schools (Jefferson 81.6%, Peabody 81.3%, Laclede 79.9%) are all on it. The gifted-only schools the plan preserves hold the five least-poor student bodies in the district.

Model D: the sensitivity test

Model D is our analytical hypothetical, not a district proposal: everything Model C does, plus closing Betty Wheeler, Mallinckrodt, and McKinley. Those three buildings hold roughly a third of the district's White students and an eighth of its non-poor students, but 3% of its Black students and 1.3% of its poor students. Adding them flips the racial disparity outright, and merely equalizes the poverty disparity. The asymmetry is the point: it takes closing every gifted-only school just to make the plan poverty-neutral, so any closure list that spares the selective schools necessarily lands on the district's poorest children.

Receiving pathways inherit the gradient

Model C rebuilds the neighborhood system as 21 PK-8 schools feeding five high school zones. The poverty of the receiving PK-8 sites runs exactly parallel to their racial makeup: the two North Side pathways that are ~100% Black are also the poorest, while the most diverse pathway (Gateway STEM) is the least poor.

Method & sources

  • Direct certification, enrollment, race counts: NCES Common Core of Data, School Characteristics layer (SY 2024-25), all schools under LEAID 2929280, queried July 22, 2026. Six schools spot-checked against NCES school detail pages.
  • Plan details, models, closure lists, capacity, projections: "Future Ready SLPS: School Portfolio Preview" (June 29, 2026) and the SLPS dashboard extract in ModelC_SchoolData.csv.
  • Denominators: NCES October 2024 membership (incl. PK) differs from SLPS dashboard 2025 enrollment and from 26-27 projections; percentages here use NCES denominators unless noted.
  • Direct certification undercounts total economic disadvantage (USDA FRL-equivalence multiplier ~1.6x). It measures benefits-linked deep poverty.
  • Record notes: Pamoja = NCES "Cole Elem."; Gateway STEM = "Gateway High"; Clyde C Miller = "Miller Career Academy"; Carnahan = "Carnahan Middle"; Gateway Michael = "Elias Michael Elem."; Nottingham CAJT = "Community Access Job Training". Madison and Beaumont report no usable CCD data. Griscom and Nahed Chapman are district schools outside the Model C dataset.
